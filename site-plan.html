Stats

Jimmer Fredette's College Basketball Stats Jimmer Fredette's college basketball career is legendary, marked by remarkable performances, record-breaking statistics, and unforgettable moments on the court. As one of the most prolific scorers in NCAA history, Fredette's statistics during his time at Brigham Young University (BYU) continue to inspire awe and admiration among basketball enthusiasts. Scoring Prowess One of the most notable aspects of Jimmer Fredette's college career is his scoring ability. Known for his uncanny knack for putting the ball in the basket, Fredette consistently lit up the scoreboard and left opposing defenses scrambling to contain him. During his senior year at BYU, Fredette led the nation in scoring, averaging an astonishing 28.9 points per game. Three-Point Shooting Fredette's scoring prowess wasn't limited to driving to the basket; he was also deadly from beyond the arc. Renowned for his long-range shooting ability, Fredette was a constant threat from three-point range, stretching defenses and forcing them to guard him closely even when he was several feet beyond the arc. His accuracy and range from downtown were a key component of his offensive arsenal, and he finished his college career with an impressive career three-point shooting percentage of over 40%. Assists and Playmaking While Fredette was primarily known as a scorer, he also displayed exceptional playmaking skills during his college career. As the focal point of BYU's offense, Fredette was tasked with creating opportunities for his teammates as well as scoring himself. His court vision, passing ability, and basketball IQ enabled him to effectively distribute the ball and orchestrate the offense, making him a dual threat as both a scorer and facilitator. Awards and Accolades Fredette's outstanding performance on the court during his college career earned him numerous awards and accolades. In addition to leading the nation in scoring, he was named the Naismith College Player of the Year, AP Player of the Year, and consensus first-team All-American. These honors solidified his status as one of the most dominant players in college basketball history and cemented his legacy as a BYU legend.
